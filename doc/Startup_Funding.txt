Startup funding is the process through which new businesses raise capital to develop products, hire employees, expand operations, and enter markets. Funding enables startups to pursue growth opportunities before they generate sufficient revenue to sustain themselves.

Many startups begin with bootstrapping, where founders use personal savings or business revenue. Friends and family financing may provide additional early support. Angel investors are individuals who invest capital in exchange for equity ownership. Venture capital firms invest in high-growth companies and often participate in multiple funding rounds.

Common funding stages include pre-seed, seed, Series A, Series B, and later-stage rounds. Pre-seed funding supports idea validation and initial product development. Seed funding helps startups build products and acquire early customers. Series A funding often focuses on scaling operations and refining business models. Later rounds support expansion, market penetration, and international growth.

Investors evaluate startups based on factors such as market opportunity, product differentiation, revenue potential, management quality, and competitive positioning. Financial metrics, customer growth, and business traction are important indicators during fundraising discussions.

Fundraising offers advantages beyond capital. Investors may provide mentorship, strategic guidance, industry connections, and recruitment support. However, founders must consider dilution because issuing equity reduces ownership percentages. Effective communication, realistic planning, and transparent reporting help build investor confidence.

Startup ecosystems include accelerators, incubators, government programs, and networking communities that support entrepreneurship. Successful fundraising requires preparation, strong storytelling, market understanding, and evidence of execution capability.
