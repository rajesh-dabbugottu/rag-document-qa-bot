Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, applications, and data from digital attacks. As organizations increasingly rely on digital infrastructure, cybersecurity has become a critical business function. Cyber threats can lead to financial losses, operational disruption, reputational damage, and legal consequences.

Common cyber threats include malware, ransomware, phishing, denial-of-service attacks, insider threats, and credential theft. Malware refers to malicious software designed to damage or compromise systems. Ransomware encrypts files and demands payment for recovery. Phishing attacks trick users into revealing sensitive information. Attackers often exploit software vulnerabilities, weak passwords, or human error.

A comprehensive cybersecurity strategy includes prevention, detection, response, and recovery. Preventive controls include firewalls, endpoint protection, multi-factor authentication, and secure configuration management. Detection capabilities involve monitoring systems for suspicious activity using logs, alerts, and threat intelligence. Incident response plans help organizations contain and remediate security events quickly. Recovery processes ensure that operations can resume after an incident.

Security awareness training is an important component of defense. Employees who understand common attack techniques are less likely to fall victim to phishing or social engineering. Regular software updates and vulnerability management reduce exposure to known threats.

Organizations often adopt frameworks and standards such as risk management programs, security policies, and compliance controls. Data protection measures include encryption, access control, and backup strategies. As technology evolves, cybersecurity professionals must continuously adapt to new attack methods and emerging risks.

Cybersecurity is not solely a technical issue. It also involves governance, legal considerations, and organizational culture.
